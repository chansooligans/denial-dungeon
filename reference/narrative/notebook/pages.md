# Notebook pages

Twelve pages, one per level (with L1 carrying three to set up
the world). The actual page text gets drafted here as we build
out each level. Each page is short — 60–150 words. Dense, warm,
not over-explanatory.

For Dana's voice / tonal anchor, see
[`../characters/dana.md`](../characters/dana.md).
For when each page is *delivered*, see
[`reveal-timeline.md`](reveal-timeline.md).

## Outline

| Level | Page | Subject | Tone / hook |
|---|---|---|---|
| L1 | 1 | The gap noticed you | Quiet, conspiratorial. *"Most people in this building have learned not to."* |
| L1 | 2 | What the Waiting Room is | Cosmology stated plainly |
| L1 | 3 | Two truths | Philosophy stated plainly. The line about bleeding vs billing. |
| L2 | 4 | The first hand-off | Where a person becomes a number. Eligibility as the moment of translation. |
| L3 | 5 | About waiting | *"The 278 is just permission to begin grieving in the right order."* |
| L4 | 6 | Chart vs. bill | Specificity. The page where the handwriting *first* drifts subtly. |
| L5 | 7 | Reading the rules that govern care | LCDs as scripture nobody reads |
| L6 | 8 | The chute | Claims leaving the building. Handwriting more agitated; signoff appears. |
| L7 | 9 | Verdicts | *"Whose courtroom is this, anyway?"* Two inks — first marginalia. |
| L8 | 10 | Bills landing in hands | The most personal page. Patient-side weight. |
| L9 | 11 | Chicken | Payers passing the bill. The marginal hand interrupts mid-sentence. |
| L10 | 12 | (the reveal) | Written from inside. ⚠ provisional with the ending. |

## Drafts

### Page 1 — *the gap noticed you*

> *"If you're reading this, the gap has noticed you. That's not
> a bad thing. It means you're paying attention. Most people in
> this building have learned not to.*
>
> *I'm going to write down what I know. Some of it will only
> make sense later. Read it anyway — twice if you need to.
> There's no shame in being slow about this."*

**Tonal anchor.** Everything else should sound like it could
come from this voice. (See [`../characters/dana.md`](../characters/dana.md).)

### Page 2 — *what the Waiting Room is*

> *"Once you start seeing it, you can't stop.*
>
> *A claim that was on the bottom of the pile yesterday is on
> the top this morning, in the same handwriting it had three
> years ago. The patient on chair 14 has been on chair 14 since
> before the chairs were rearranged. The same name comes up on
> a denial three times in a year, only the first one was real.*
>
> *Most people in this building have learned to look past it.
> I don't blame them. It's safer.*
>
> *Some claims don't resolve — the patient gives up, the payer
> wins, the appeal never lands. They go somewhere. I won't tell
> you where; you'll see it. Just know that every chair in there
> has a name on it. Somebody put it there."*

**Authoring notes:** Cosmology stated through observation, not
exposition. Three specific surreal details (the recurring claim,
chair 14, the three-time denial) — picked because they're both
reasonably explicable in a normal hospital *and* unmistakable
once you've seen the Waiting Room. The "I don't blame them" is
Dana's empathy. The final line implies *fault* without pointing
fingers, which is the metaphor's working temperature.

### Page 3 — *two truths*

> *"Here's the rule that keeps this work from breaking you:*
>
> *There's the truth that bleeds, and there's the truth that
> bills. The hospital can't run without both. Most days you only
> have to hold one. Don't trust anyone who says they always know
> which is which.*
>
> *The doctor I worked with last week saved someone's life and
> coded the wrong dx. Both true. The payer denied the claim
> correctly per policy and the policy is cruel. Both true.*
>
> *Your job — your real job, not the one in the manual — is to
> hold both at once without letting either disappear. Most
> people learn to hold one. Don't be most people."*

**Authoring notes:** Philosophy delivered through two grounded
examples, not abstraction. The first (doctor + wrong dx) shows
the *technical* both-truths; the second (payer + cruel policy)
shows the *moral* both-truths. "Don't be most people" is the
warm prescription — Dana's not lecturing the player, she's
recruiting them.

### Page 4 — *the first hand-off*

> *"Registration is where it begins.*
>
> *A person walks in. They have a face, a story, a reason
> they're here. Someone behind a counter asks for their card.
> Types something. Hits enter. A query goes out. A response
> comes back. By the time they sit down in the waiting area,
> they're an ID number on a screen.*
>
> *That's not bad. It's necessary. Insurance is mostly numbers.
> But it's a hand-off, and a hand-off is where things break.*
>
> *Watch the front desk. Watch what gets typed and what gets
> verified. Run the 270 even when the card looks fine. The 271
> doesn't lie; the card photocopy might."*

**Authoring notes:** The level-2 teaching point (270/271
eligibility) delivered as practical advice inside a metaphor
(person → number). "It's not bad. It's necessary." prevents the
metaphor from being moralistic — Dana isn't condemning the
hand-off, she's noticing where it breaks.

### Page 5 — *about waiting*

> *"Prior auth is a strange thing. The payer asks you to ask
> permission to do something the doctor already decided was
> needed. Three days later they say yes (probably) or no
> (sometimes) or 'we need more information' (usually).*
>
> *The 278 is just permission to begin grieving in the right
> order.*
>
> *I'm not telling you to like it. I'm telling you to file it
> before the procedure, every time, even when the patient is in
> front of you bleeding. Because if you don't, the claim goes
> to a place down the hall where nothing ever gets resolved,
> and the patient ends up paying. That's the math. Sorry."*

**Authoring notes:** The grief-line is the page's spine.
"That's the math. Sorry." is Dana refusing to soften the
critique — she names the system as cruel and tells the player
to file the form anyway. That's the wise+warm voice in tension:
empathetic *and* practical. Don't lose either side.

### Page 6 — *chart vs. bill*

> *"Coding is translation. Not metaphor — actual translation.
> The clinician writes 'heart failure' and a coder turns it
> into I50.42 (or I50.9, or I11.0, depending). Each translation
> is a choice. Each choice is defensible or it isn't.*
>
> *The most common mistake isn't bad coding. It's coding from a
> chart that was too vague to start with. CDI is upstream of
> all of this — get the documentation right and the codes*

**Authoring notes:** ⚠ **The page cuts off here, mid-sentence.**
This is the L4 first-cracks moment from
[`reveal-timeline.md`](reveal-timeline.md). The missing rest of
the sentence ("...the codes follow.") is gone — Dana stopped
writing. The empty bottom half of the page is the foreshadow.

What's missing might never be recovered, or might appear later
as wet ink, or might be the player's first glimpse that
something is happening to Dana. Author's choice when wiring up.

### Page 7 — *reading the rules that govern care*

> *"There's a document called an LCD — Local Coverage
> Determination. It says, in detail, the conditions under which
> a payer will cover a given service. There's another called an
> NCD. There are payer policy bulletins. There are NCCI edits.
> There are contract attachments with fee schedules buried five
> layers deep.*
>
> *Almost nobody reads them. Doctors don't. Patients can't.
> Most billers skim them when something denies. They are
> written in the dialect of legal, medical, and accounting at
> once, and they govern whether a person gets a thing they
> need.*
>
> *Read the LCD. Read the policy. The rules the system follows
> aren't a secret — they're just boring. Boring is a kind of
> hiding."*

**Authoring notes:** "Boring is a kind of hiding" is the
keeper line — the philosophical observation that explains why
opaque systems stay opaque without anyone deciding it. The list
of documents (LCD, NCD, NCCI, contract attachments) is dense
on purpose — Dana respects the player enough to use the real
words.

### Page 8 — *the chute*

> *"Once a claim leaves the building it's no longer yours. The
> 837 goes to the clearinghouse, the clearinghouse runs its
> edits, the clearinghouse sends a 277CA back if anything
> broke, the rest get forwarded to the payer. There are vendors
> in this chain you've never met making decisions about whose
> work survives.*
>
> *Things I've watched fall off claims at this stage: rendering
> provider taxonomy. Diagnosis pointers. Modifier 25. Whole
> service lines. None of it on purpose. All of it because
> somebody's edit table was old.*
>
> *Trust the scrubber and verify. Trust the clearinghouse and
> verify. The chute has teeth.*
>
> *— Dana"*

**Authoring notes:** First page Dana signs her name. Subtle
wrongness — she's never signed before. Read against the L4
mid-sentence cut-off, the signoff feels like she's worried this
might be the last one she gets to write. Don't underline this;
let the player feel it.

### Page 9 — *verdicts*

> *"The 835 lands. Adjustment codes attached: CO, OA, PR,
> sometimes PI. CARC, RARC, the math beneath. You read them
> like a verdict.*
>
> *Whose courtroom is this anyway? Nobody appointed the payer
> to be a judge. The contract did. We signed it. We needed the
> network. The signature was years ago and the judge has
> changed three times since.*
>
> *Read the remit twice. The first read tells you what they
> paid. The second read tells you why. The why is where you
> fight back, if you're going to fight back. And — listen —
> sometimes the right answer is to write it off. That's also a
> verdict. Yours."*

> *— margin annotation, smaller hand:*
> *"Sometimes you write it off because the patient died. Don't
> forget that's a verdict too."*

**Authoring notes:** First marginal annotation in a different
hand. The margin voice is *adjacent* to Dana's — not unlike,
not identical. The reveal-timeline calls this "two inks." The
content of the margin is heavier than what's on the page —
that's the device. The marginal voice will become important
later (page 11, page 12).

### Page 10 — *bills landing in hands*

> *"This is the page I never wanted to write.*
>
> *Eventually the bill lands in someone's mailbox. They open
> it. They look at the number. They feel something — fear,
> anger, resignation, sometimes relief that it's not worse.
> Sometimes worse than fear.*
>
> *Most of what we do here decides what number lands in that
> mailbox. Most of the time we don't think about it that way.
> We think about denial rates and AR aging and clean-claim
> percentages. Those are real. But the number on the statement
> is also real, and the person opening it is also real.*
>
> *Screen for charity care. Offer payment plans. Don't be the
> reason someone files for bankruptcy. There are people who
> walk in here scared, and they don't always know how to ask
> for what they need. You can be the one who tells them."*

**Authoring notes:** The most emotional page. Opens with the
slip ("the page I never wanted to write") — Dana being more
vulnerable than she usually allows. Lands the patient-side
weight without specifying a particular patient (per request to
keep Dana's specific child case flexible). The closing
prescription is Dana at her most maternal: "you can be the one
who tells them."

### Page 11 — *chicken*

> *"Coordination of benefits is a polite phrase for a game of
> chicken between two insurance companies who both think the
> other one should pay.*
>
> *The patient is in the middle. The hospital is in the middle.
> The contract chain says who's primary, who's secondary, who's
> tertiary. Most of the time the chain is correct on file.
> Sometimes it isn't. When it isn't, claims sit. When claims
> sit, money doesn't move. When money doesn't move, somebody —*

> *— margin annotation, the same smaller hand:*
> *"— eventually forgets there was a person attached to the
> claim at all."*

> *I was going to write that. She's right.*
>
> *— D."*

**Authoring notes:** The marginal voice interrupts mid-sentence
and Dana finishes the thought *by acknowledging the interruption*.
The signoff has shrunk from "— Dana" (page 8) to "— D." Subtle
but consistent.

The "she" in "she's right" is deliberately ambiguous. Could be
the player's apprenticeship voice annotating now. Could be
Dana-from-inside annotating Dana-from-outside. We resolve it
on page 12 — or don't.

### Page 12 — *(the reveal)*

> ⚠ **PROVISIONAL.** This page lands at L10 and is tightly
> coupled to the ending, which is also provisional (see
> [`../endings.md`](../endings.md)). Draft below; revise when
> the ending lands. Voice and direction are the most stable
> parts; specifics about Dana's child case are intentionally
> *not* in this draft so we can revisit her case later without
> rewriting the page.

> *"You found me.*
>
> *I was hoping you would. I was hoping you wouldn't. Both can
> be true. You've already learned that.*
>
> *Whatever happens at the audit — whatever you do with the
> file in front of you — I want you to know I left this on
> purpose. The unfinished pages, the wet ink, the chair I left
> at my desk. The notebook was a map. You followed it.*
>
> *If you take me with you when you leave, I'll come. If you
> can't, I understand. The work doesn't stop either way. Someone
> after you will pick up the notebook. Be the kind of senior I
> tried to be. Be warmer than I was.*
>
> *I love you, kid.*
>
> *— D."*

**Authoring notes:** The voice on this page is Dana's — the
"smaller hand" from the margins is now the main hand, because
Dana-on-this-side and Dana-on-the-other-side have collapsed
into one self. (That's the "the voice changed; the kindness
didn't" timeline note made literal.)

"I love you, kid" is the cracked-open warmth at the end —
earned by the run, not earlier. "Be warmer than I was" is
humility — Dana doesn't claim to have done this perfectly.

No specifics about her child case in this draft. When we
revisit her case, the version of this page that gets shipped
might fold a sentence in (or might not).

## Voice consistency — on-voice / off-voice

When drafting future pages or annotations, sanity-check against
these.

**On-voice (Dana):**

- *"Boring is a kind of hiding."*
- *"That's the math. Sorry."*
- *"Don't be most people."*
- *"Trust the scrubber and verify."*
- *"You can be the one who tells them."*

What these have in common: short sentences, observation
followed by direction, no condescension, no preening, occasional
warmth that earns the next hard thing.

**Off-voice (revise):**

- *"As we will see in section 4 of the manual..."* (lecture)
- *"They're all crooks!"* (cynicism without specificity)
- *"You've got this!"* (deflection / cheerleading)
- *"Healthcare is broken."* (abstraction)
- *"This is so unfair."* (pure complaint)

These break either the warmth (1, 4) or the wisdom (2, 3, 5)
or both. Dana doesn't lecture, doesn't generalize, doesn't
emote without grounding.

**Marginal hand voice** (pages 9, 11, then page 12 absorbs it):

- Closer to the ground than Dana usually allows herself.
- Says the thing Dana was about to say but couldn't.
- Slightly more vulnerable, slightly more direct.
- Same wisdom; different temperature.

## Authoring notes (general)

- Each page is short. Not a chapter. A page.
- Voice is wise + warm. Not preachy. Not flippant.
- Specific images beat abstractions every time. ("Chair 14"
  beats "the unresolved.")
- Don't explain the cosmology more than once. Page 2 says it;
  later pages live inside it.
- Late pages (6+) carry small wrongnesses — sentences that
  end mid-thought, signoffs that change shape, dates that
  drift. Build the reveal through small wrongness, not
  announcements.
- Wet ink + marginalia are limited tools. Use the cut-off
  (page 6), the signoff (page 8), the margin (page 9, 11),
  and the absorption (page 12). Don't overplay any of them.
