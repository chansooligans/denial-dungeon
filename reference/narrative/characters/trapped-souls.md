# Trapped souls — the others

Dana is the most prominent, but she's not alone. Throughout the
Waiting Room are others who tried to fix something specific and
got stuck.

They are *not* a chorus of saints. Each tried something
particular and lost something particular. None are
uncomplicated.

## Two design rules to keep this from going cheesy

1. **Each trapped soul tried *one specific thing*.** Not "the
   system" abstractly. Not "everyone should have healthcare."
   A specific contract. A specific policy. A specific
   spreadsheet. The specificity is the truth.
2. **Not all of them want to leave.** At least one is *happier*
   in the Waiting Room than she was in the hospital — the
   system stopped asking her to compromise. Players who try to
   "save" her find her uninterested. That ambivalence is the
   un-cheesy move.

## Player encounters

Player encounters with these are **optional, off-the-spine
content**. Each is a small character beat that deepens the
cosmology without requiring narrative gymnastics.

The CFO's "no" is the most important — it means the game has a
thesis about ambivalence, not just rescue. Some people *chose*
the form-truth over the people-truth. Some chose to stay in the
place where compromise stops being asked of them. We don't
condemn that. We let the player find her, hear her out, and
walk away.

## Are these souls retrievable on different terms than Dana?

Maybe yes — maybe each requires a *different kind of
resolution*:

- **The Underpayment Analyst** is freed when the player
  recovers an actual underpayment, vindicating her tracker.
- **The Patient Advocate** is freed when a fair payment plan is
  signed.
- **The Coding Manager** — unclear. Maybe by writing a
  defensible chart against productivity pressure?
- **The CFO** is *not* freed at all. She's the
  un-cheesy move.

Each freeing is a side-quest with cosmological coherence (a
specific kind of unresolved-ness gets resolved). To be designed
when these characters get authored.
